Frequently Asked Questions
Is LEVERAGE case management software?

No. Case management organizes files. LEVERAGE calculates damages, tracks SOL deadlines, checks demand readiness, and projects settlement ranges. It works alongside Clio, Filevine, or whatever you use — it doesn't replace it.

Does LEVERAGE write demand letters?

No. LEVERAGE gives you the numbers and runs a demand-readiness checklist before you send. You write every word. By writing the letter yourself and using LEVERAGE as a calculator and checklist, you reduce AI-drafting risk while keeping attorney review and judgment in control. LEVERAGE is a calculator and checklist, not a ghostwriter.

How does the pricing work?

$99/month with INTAKE. $349/month standalone. One plan. All features. No per-case billing. No overage charges. Hard gate at 22 active cases. If you consistently exceed 22, we'll discuss upgrading. Most solo and small PI firms never hit the gate.

How is the damages number calculated?

Example working formula: Medical bills + Lost wages + (Pain & Suffering Multiplier × Medicals) + Future costs = Gross Damages. Settlement range = 60%-85% of gross. The multiplier is your judgment call. LEVERAGE warns if it exceeds 2.5x but doesn't override your decision. Same inputs always produce the same output — the math is auditable.

Does LEVERAGE provide legal advice?

No. LEVERAGE is a calculator and checklist — decision support, not legal advice. You remain in control of every case decision, every demand letter, and every settlement negotiation.

How does LEVERAGE work with INTAKE?

If you use INTAKE, structured case data can flow into LEVERAGE, reducing manual re-entry. You also get the ecosystem price ($99/mo instead of $349). LEVERAGE also works standalone if you enter case facts manually.

What data does LEVERAGE store?

Structured case signals — incident type, state, medical totals, wage loss, insurer name. No medical records. No client identifiers. No document content. The compliance engine runs on structured signals, not document uploads. This is by design — ABA Rule 1.6 (confidentiality) is easier to satisfy when sensitive documents never leave your possession.
